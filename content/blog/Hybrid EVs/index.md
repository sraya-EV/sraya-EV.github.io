One of the biggest challenges in cancer nanomedicine is getting drugs to tumor cells efficiently and selectively — without harming healthy tissue. In this work published in *Acta Biomaterialia*, we tackled this by engineering a new class of nanoparticle we called a **hybrid exosome (HE)**: a fusion of natural macrophage-derived small extracellular vesicles (sEVs) and synthetic liposomes.

## The Problem with Natural Exosomes

Natural sEVs are exciting for drug delivery because they carry the biological identity of their parent cells — in this case, immune macrophage cells that naturally seek out tumors. However, sEVs have two major drawbacks:

- **Poor yield**: isolating enough sEVs for therapeutic use is difficult
- **Instability**: sEVs lose functional properties during isolation and storage

Synthetic liposomes, on the other hand, are easy to produce in large quantities and highly stable, but they lack the biological targeting ability of natural EVs.

## Our Solution: Hybrid Exosomes

We hybridized macrophage-derived sEVs with synthetic liposomes using a membrane extrusion technique — essentially fusing the two together to create a best-of-both-worlds nanoplatform. The resulting hybrid exosomes (177 ± 21 nm) retained key EV marker proteins (CD81, CD63, CD9, CD11b) while showing significantly improved colloidal stability over 30 days compared to native sEVs.

We confirmed successful hybridization using fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET), showing a 27% decrement in FRET efficiency after fusion — direct evidence of membrane integration.

## Selective Tumor Targeting

The key question was: does the hybrid exosome inherit the tumor-targeting properties of macrophages?

Confocal imaging and flow cytometry on cancer cells (4T1 breast cancer, K7M2 osteosarcoma) vs. normal fibroblasts (NIH/3T3) gave a clear answer — **yes**. HE was internalized 3–4 times more efficiently by tumor cells compared to plain liposomes, while showing no significant difference in normal cells. When we digested the surface proteins of sEVs with Proteinase K, internalization dropped by ~5-fold, confirming that EV surface proteins are driving the selective tumor targeting.

## Drug Loading and Cancer Killing

We loaded doxorubicin (DOX) into HE with a high efficiency of 99% at the optimal input concentration. HE-DOX showed pH-sensitive drug release — releasing 83% of drug in acidic conditions (mimicking the tumor microenvironment) vs. 55% at physiological pH — a desirable property for cancer-specific drug delivery.

In cytotoxicity assays, HE-DOX showed significantly enhanced potency against 4T1 breast cancer cells (IC50 = 0.82 µM) compared to free DOX (IC50 = 3.16 µM), while maintaining similar efficacy to free DOX in other cell lines. Most importantly, HE-DOX showed selective cytotoxicity toward tumor cells over normal fibroblasts.

## Why This Matters

This work demonstrates that hybridizing immune cell-derived EVs with synthetic liposomes is a viable strategy to:

- Overcome the yield and stability limitations of natural exosomes
- Retain and leverage the intrinsic tumor-targeting properties of immune cells
- Achieve pH-responsive, cancer-selective drug delivery

These results lay a foundation for scalable, biomimetic EV-based drug delivery platforms for cancer therapeutics.
